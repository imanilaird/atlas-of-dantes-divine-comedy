The Project

Dante’s Comedy is shaped by the structure of the three realms of afterlife — Inferno, Purgatorio, and Paradiso — conceived of according to late medieval geography, cosmology, and theology. However, the construction of fictional spaces is just one aspect of Dante’s geographical imagination. The path through the afterlife is full of references to places in the physical world, albeit that of the 14th century.

Mapping Dante, a project developed by Andrea Gazzoni at the University of Pennsylvania, was original in that it used a map to visualize the places Dante mentions and observe their distribution both in the “real” world and in the course of his long poem. However, the 2017 project is now defunct, and the Atlas of Dante’s Divine Comedy fills in the gap it left. It is unique in that it is a living project, continuously gathering more geographical references, while being stored on GitHub, the world’s most popular open-source platform that allows anyone to view its code.

Unlike the static nature of traditional maps and lists that limits the potential of literary cartography, this interactive map has the opportunity to create visualizations that are multi-layered and dynamic. Digital literary mapping can enhance our approach to a text by extracting a network of references which, in turn, become part of the larger network of the “real” world’s geography.
